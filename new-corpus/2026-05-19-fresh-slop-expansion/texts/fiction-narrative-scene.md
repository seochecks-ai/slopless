# The Bell Under Glass

Mara reached the upper landing as the last candle in the corridor gave a blue shiver and went out.

She stopped. She looked into the darkness. The darkness looked back, heavy and waiting and full of the feeling of a room that had remembered something. Her breath caught in her throat. Her chest tightened. Her fingers tightened around the brass key Master Orlen had pressed into her palm before he died.

Behind her, Tovin stepped onto the landing one stair at a time. He looked down the corridor. He looked at Mara. He looked back down the corridor again.

"It changed," he said.

"The hallway?"

"The silence."

Mara listened. The tower had been full of sounds all night. Rain on slate. Wind against stone. The water wheels below the cliff turning in the black river. Now there was nothing. The silence was not empty. It pressed against her ears with two invisible hands.

Fear moved through her like cold honey. It filled her arms and legs. It gathered behind her eyes.

She took one step forward.

The floorboard answered with a soft cry.

Tovin flinched. Mara flinched too, and hated that she did. The air changed around them. It became colder first, then warmer, then colder again. It had the feeling of someone having just left the room, except no one had left.

"We can still go back," Tovin said.

Mara looked at the key. The teeth were shaped like three small moons. She looked at the corridor. She looked at the black windows on the left, each reflecting a pale version of her face. She looked at the locked red door at the far end. It had not been there when they climbed the tower.

That was the problem. That was the thing that mattered. She knew that now.

"No," she said. "We cannot."

They walked.

Mara stepped. Tovin stepped after her. The corridor seemed to lengthen with every step. The right wall was carved with old saints whose faces had been scratched away. The windows on the left showed the storm outside, except the storm had stopped making noise. Lightning opened and closed far beyond the glass without thunder.

She looked at the first saint. No face.

She looked at the second saint. No face.

She looked at the third saint.

Its face was wet.

Mara stopped. Her heart jumped hard. Tovin bumped into her shoulder.

"What?" he whispered.

Water was running from the blank stone where the saint's eyes should have been. It gathered under the chin and fell drop by drop onto the floor.

Drip.

Drip.

Drip.

"Stone does not weep," Tovin said.

"This tower does."

Mara did not know why she said it. The sentence came from somewhere below thought. When she heard herself say it, she understood that it was true, or close enough to true to be dangerous.

They moved again.

The dripping followed them for five steps. Then it stopped.

The air changed its mind. It had gone from watching to remembering. Mara felt the difference on her tongue. It tasted like copper and old flowers.

She looked back.

The stairs were gone.

Where the landing had been, there was now a wall of smooth black stone.

Tovin stepped backward and put both hands against it. He pushed once, then twice. Nothing moved. His breathing became quick. Panic came off him like heat.

"Mara."

"I see it."

"The stairs are gone."

"I see it."

"That means we are trapped."

She wanted to tell him there was always another passage, another latch, another servant stair. But the words would not form. The truth sat between them like a third person.

They were trapped.

The realization struck her as simply as a door closing. The tower was not hiding a mystery from them. The tower had become the mystery.

She turned toward the red door.

"Then forward," she said.

They walked faster. The red door came closer, but not steadily. Sometimes it seemed ten steps away. Sometimes it seemed a hundred. Mara kept her eyes on it because looking away felt dangerous. She looked at the iron hinges. She looked at the painted panels. She looked at the round brass lock. The lock had the same three small moons as the key.

Her palm hurt around the key.

The memory of Orlen came back with painful clarity. He had lain on the library floor with silver dust on his beard and blood darkening his robe. His eyes had not looked at the wound. They had looked at Mara. He had put the key in her hand. His fingers had been cold, but the key had been hot.

"The bell is under glass," he had whispered. "Do not trust the widow in blue."

Then he had looked past her at something that was not there, and terror had filled his face so completely that it had seemed to become his face. After that he was gone.

Mara had not understood.

Now she still did not understand.

But she was beginning to.

That was what frightened her most.

They reached the door. The red paint was not paint. It was some kind of lacquer, dark and glossy, and shapes moved under it. They shifted slowly, like thoughts trying to become memories.

Tovin leaned close, then leaned back at once. "There are faces in it."

Mara did not want to look. She looked anyway.

There were faces. Some old. Some young. Some with mouths open. Some with no mouths at all. They floated under the red surface, pressed there, looking out with desperate patience. One of them turned toward her.

It had Master Orlen's eyes.

Mara's body went cold, then hot, then empty. Grief rose inside her so sharply that she almost bent around it. It was not a feeling. It was a blade. It slid between her ribs and made every breath careful.

"No," she said.

Tovin touched her sleeve. "Do not."

But Mara was already lifting the key.

The lock waited.

The key slid in.

At once the whole corridor breathed.

The saints leaned forward with their missing faces. The windows flashed white. The floor under Mara's boots became soft for one impossible second, as if she had stepped onto mud instead of stone. Tovin grabbed her arm. She grabbed the key. The brass was burning now. Pain went through her palm. She gasped but did not let go.

The lock turned by itself.

Click.

The red door opened inward.

Beyond it was a room that should not have fit inside the tower.

It was round, wide, and high, with a ceiling lost in darkness. A ring of tall mirrors stood along the wall, each covered in gray cloth. In the center of the room was a glass case on a black pedestal. Inside the case was a small silver bell.

Mara stepped in.

Tovin stepped in after her.

The door closed behind them without a sound.

The silence changed again. It became softer. It became almost kind. That made it worse. A thin blue light came from nowhere and lay across the floor in strips. The bell sat under glass, bright and waiting.

"The bell," Tovin said.

"Under glass," Mara said.

"Then Orlen was right."

"Dying men can be right."

"They can also be wrong."

Mara did not answer. She walked toward the pedestal. Each step sounded too plain. Boot on stone. Boot on stone. Boot on stone. Nothing stopped her. No hand came from the shadows. No hidden blade struck from the floor. The emptiness felt arranged. The lack of danger felt like another kind of danger.

She looked at the mirrors. The cloths over them did not move, but she had the strong feeling that something behind them was looking out.

"We should leave them covered," Tovin said.

"Yes."

Halfway to the pedestal, the first cloth fell.

It did not slide. It dropped straight down, like a body.

The mirror beneath it did not show the room. It showed the library as it had been the night before. Shelves split. Candles overturned. Silver dust floating in the air. Master Orlen on the floor. Mara saw herself kneeling beside him. She saw Tovin at the door, frozen. She saw the blue-cloaked woman standing behind the long table.

Mara stared.

The widow in blue.

The woman in the mirror turned her head and looked directly at Mara.

Mara's mouth went dry. The woman had a narrow face, calm eyes, and hair the color of old ash. Her blue veil covered her shoulders like water. In the real library Mara had seen no one. In the mirror, the woman had been there all along.

"Who is she?" Tovin whispered.

"I do not know."

The woman in blue raised one finger to her lips.

Then the mirror went black.

Mara stepped back. She stepped into Tovin. He steadied her. Shame flashed through her, hot and quick, then vanished under fear. She looked at the other covered mirrors. There were eleven. No, twelve. She counted again. Thirteen. The number would not hold still.

"We take the bell and leave," Tovin said.

"How?"

"Break the glass."

Mara looked at the case. The bell was small enough to fit in one hand. Its handle was carved like a sleeping bird. No dust lay on the glass. The pedestal had no lock, no clasp, no seam.

"Orlen said the bell is under glass," she said. "He did not say to break it."

"He did not say much else because he died."

The words came out harsh. Tovin regretted them at once. Mara saw regret cross his face like a gray wash behind his eyes. He looked away.

"I know," she said.

Another cloth fell.

This mirror showed the village below the tower. The streets were empty. Doors hung open. The well in the square steamed in the rain. At the center of the square stood Lysa, the miller's daughter, holding a lantern. Behind her, the villagers stood in every doorway.

None of them blinked.

Tovin made a broken sound. "My mother."

Mara looked harder. There, in the doorway of the cooper's house, stood Tovin's mother with her hands folded in front of her apron. Her eyes were open too wide.

The mirror darkened.

Tovin stepped toward it.

Mara caught his arm. "No."

"They are down there."

"That is not down there."

"You do not know that."

"Neither do you."

Anger moved between them, sudden and red. It was almost visible in the air. It wrapped around their wrists. It pulled at their words. Mara felt it and hated it, because anger was easier than fear.

Tovin pulled his arm free but did not go to the mirror.

The room waited.

The bell waited too.

Mara forced herself to look at the pedestal again. She walked the last steps to it. The glass case showed her reflection bent around the bell. Her face looked pale and older than it had that morning. Behind her reflection, in the curve of the glass, she saw the covered mirrors. Behind those, she saw a blue shape.

She turned.

No one was there.

She looked back at the glass.

The blue shape was gone.

Her hands shook. She put them behind her back. Then she realized Tovin's hands were shaking too. That helped and did not help.

"There must be writing," she said.

She circled the pedestal. There was writing at the base, but it was not in any language she knew. The letters were thin and hooked. They seemed to rearrange when she looked at them. She blinked. The letters became scratches.

One mark repeated at the start of every line. Three small moons. The key's mark. The lock's mark. The bell's mark, maybe. It was not proof, but it was a pattern. Patterns mattered. Orlen had taught her that when she was nine and convinced ghosts moved the ink bottles in the archive.

"A haunting is a pattern without a witness," he had said. "Find the witness, and you find the hand."

She had thought he meant there was always a trick.

Now she realized he had meant something else.

There was always a witness.

The realization opened inside her like a trapdoor. She looked at the mirrors. She looked at the bell. She looked at the key burn mark in her palm.

"The mirrors are witnesses," she said.

Tovin stared at her. "What?"

"They saw what happened."

"Mirrors do not see."

"These do."

As if answering, three cloths fell at once.

The room filled with images.

In one mirror, Orlen argued with the widow in blue in the library. His hands moved sharply. Her hands stayed folded. Silver dust glittered between them like snow.

In another mirror, the same woman stood in the bell room years ago beside Lord Veyr, the old master of the tower, dead for twenty years. He was laughing. Then he was not laughing.

In the third mirror, Mara saw herself as a child, asleep in the archive with ink on her cheek. Orlen covered her with his cloak. He looked tired. He looked kind. Then he looked toward the door, and fear crossed his face.

Mara could not breathe.

Grief came again, thicker this time. It filled the room. It covered the floor. It climbed her legs. She wanted to sink into it and let it close over her head.

Tovin put a hand on her shoulder. "Mara."

She shook her head.

"Mara, look at me."

She looked at him.

He was frightened. He was also still there. That mattered. It did not solve anything, but it mattered.

"What do we do?" he asked.

The question filled her skull. She looked at the bell. She looked at the glass. She looked at the uncovered mirrors. She looked at the door, though it had vanished into the wall.

Then the bell moved.

It did not ring. It tilted slightly under the glass, as if nudged by an unseen finger. The sleeping bird handle trembled. The silver body caught the blue light and threw it onto the ceiling.

High above them, something answered.

Not a sound. A movement.

Dust fell from the dark ceiling in a slow circle. The room had no visible rafters, no beams, no dome, only blackness above. In that blackness, something enormous shifted its weight.

Tovin drew his knife.

Mara almost told him the knife would not help. She did not. Holding it helped him stand. That was enough.

"The bell wants to be rung," he said.

"Or something wants us to think that."

Another mirror uncovered itself.

This one showed Mara and Tovin in the room, from behind. It showed them standing before the glass case. It showed the ceiling above them. In the mirror, the ceiling was not empty.

A bell hung there.

Not the small silver bell under glass. A great black bell, wider than a wagon, suspended by chains that went nowhere. Its surface was cracked. From the crack leaked blue light.

Under the great bell stood the widow in blue.

She was not in the room. She was in the mirror. She looked at Mara again. Slowly, she pointed down.

Mara followed the gesture.

At the base of the pedestal was a small shadow where no shadow should be. She knelt. Her knee touched the cold stone. She reached under the lip of the base and felt a notch.

Something clicked.

The glass case lifted one inch.

Tovin sucked in a breath.

Mara froze. She had expected smoke or teeth or a scream. Nothing happened. The case hovered, held by hidden gears or by something stranger than gears.

"Do not touch it yet," Tovin said.

"I know."

But she wanted to. The wanting rose in her like thirst. It was not only hers. The bell wanted her hand. The room wanted her hand. The dead wanted her hand. The village wanted her hand. Every unseen thing in the tower leaned toward that one small motion.

"If I ring it," Mara said, "something will happen."

"Yes."

"If I do not ring it, something will also happen."

"Yes."

"That is not a choice."

"It is the kind we get."

The simple truth settled over them. It was not wise. It was not comforting. It was only true.

The uncovered mirrors brightened again. Mara saw Lord Veyr fall with his hands over his ears. She saw the widow in blue smash a mirror with a silver hammer. She saw villagers carrying sleeping children into the tower. She saw Orlen hiding the key inside a book of harvest accounts. She saw herself finding that book weeks before he died, then putting it back because it seemed boring.

Her stomach dropped.

She understood.

Not everything. Not enough. But enough to make the shape of it.

Orlen had known. He had hidden the key where only carelessness would find it. He had wanted the bell found and not found. He had been trying to solve a thing that did not want to be solved.

And the widow in blue had not killed him.

The realization came like ice water. It rearranged the room.

"She warned him," Mara whispered.

Tovin looked at the mirror where the widow stood beneath the black bell. "Who did?"

"The widow. She was warning him."

"Then who killed him?"

The black bell in the mirror swung once.

No sound came.

Every mirror cracked.

The cracks ran across the glass like white lightning. Mara staggered. Tovin grabbed the pedestal. The small silver bell shivered under the lifted case. The great black bell in the mirror swung again.

This time there was sound.

It was not loud. It was worse than loud. It was low and deep and close. It went through Mara's teeth. It went through her spine. It turned her thoughts sideways. She fell to one knee. Tovin dropped his knife and clapped his hands over his ears.

The mirrors spoke with Orlen's voice.

"Not the widow."

The words came from every cracked surface at once.

Mara lifted her head. Blood was on her lip. She did not remember biting it.

"Then who?" she said.

The room answered by changing.

The blue light went out. The darkness fell all at once. Mara heard Tovin breathing. She heard her own breathing. She heard the glass case humming above the pedestal. Then one by one, the mirrors lit from within, not with scenes but with faces.

Orlen.

Lord Veyr.

Lysa from the village.

Tovin's mother.

Dozens more.

All of them looked at Mara.

All of them opened their mouths.

"The heir," they said.

Tovin went still.

Mara turned toward him slowly.

He was looking at the faces. Then he was looking at her. Then he was looking down at his own hands.

"No," he said.

But the word had no strength. It fell apart as it left him.

Mara's mind moved quickly and badly. Tovin was the cooper's son. He had always been the cooper's son. He had grown up stealing apples and mending barrels. He could not be an heir to anything except debts and splinters.

Unless the tower had hidden him.

Unless Orlen had hidden him.

Unless everyone had.

Suspicion entered her like smoke. It curled through her chest and touched old memories until they changed shape. Tovin at the tower gate with a password he claimed he guessed. Tovin reading Veyr's old family motto though he had never learned court script. Tovin sleeping through the bell chimes that woke everyone else.

She hated the thoughts as they formed.

Another realization came, smaller and worse.

Orlen had sent for Tovin that night.

Not for help.

For proof.

Tovin saw the change in her face. Hurt flashed through him. It was so clear she almost saw it leave a mark on the air.

"Mara," he said.

The silver bell rang by itself.

It made one small clear note.

The room changed again.

Not completely. Not in a way she could explain. The mirrors stayed cracked. The pedestal stayed before her. Tovin stayed where he was. But behind him, on the floor, his shadow lifted its head.

Mara looked at it.

Tovin looked at her looking.

"What is it?" he asked.

The shadow smiled before Mara could answer.

From the corridor came a new sound.

Footsteps.

Slow steps.

Many steps.

The widow in blue appeared in the black mirror, no longer calm, no longer distant. She raised both hands against the glass from the other side. Her mouth moved. Mara could not hear the words, but she knew what they were.

Do not let it ring again.

The bell under glass trembled.

Tovin stepped back. His shadow did not step with him.

Mara's fear returned, familiar and heavy. It settled in her bones. It filled her mouth. It made her fingers tighten around the key. She looked at Tovin. She looked at the shadow. She looked at the bell under the lifted glass.

Then someone on the other side of the vanished door knocked.
